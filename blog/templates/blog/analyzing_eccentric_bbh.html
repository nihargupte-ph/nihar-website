Black holes are well, black, you can't see them with your naked eye. However, if you have two black holes orbitting each other, they release "gravitational waves". You also can't see these with your eyes but we've built detectors to find these gravitational waves. In the following blogpost, Enn and Palis will show you how to simulate a binary black hole and what the emitted waves look like. Then they will show you how given the data in the detectors what the properties of the black holes are and what these properties tell us about nature.

But in reality, we can't create binary black holes, they happen somewhere far away and we just see the data (the gray lines in the bottom two plots). Even worse, since there is noise in our detectors, we can't easily look at the data and extract the parameters of the binary black hole. It's like finding a needle (the gravitational wave) in a haystack (the data)!

Finding this needle can take a long time. We've trained a machine learning model called "DINGO" to help us find the signal in the data. This model can run in just seconds. Click below to reconstruct the signal from the data!
